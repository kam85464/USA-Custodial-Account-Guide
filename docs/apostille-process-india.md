# How to Get a Document Apostilled

If you are living abroad and need to submit a legal document (like a Power of Attorney or Successor Designation) to a U.S. brokerage, you will likely need an **Apostille certification** from your resident country's competent authority.

## General Step-by-Step Process (Example: India)

### 1. Notarization
Have your document signed and stamped by a local Notary Public in your city.

### 2. State Attestation
The document must be attested by the state's Home Department or the Sub-Divisional Magistrate (SDM). 
- **SDM Attestation** is generally faster and widely accepted for MEA Apostilles.

### 3. MEA Apostille
The Ministry of External Affairs (MEA) is the authority in India that issues Apostilles. Other countries have similar central departments.
- You cannot go to the MEA directly. You must use an **outsourced agency** (such as VFS Global, IVS Global, or BLS International).
- The agency will take your document, get the MEA sticker, and return it to you.

## Alternative: U.S. Consulate Notarization
For many U.S. brokerages, a **Notarial Service** at a U.S. Embassy or Consulate in your resident country is often accepted *instead* of an Apostille.
- **Pros:** Recognized immediately as a "U.S. Notary."
- **Cons:** Requires an appointment at the Consulate (can be hard to get) and costs ~$50 per seal.

## Which one should I choose?
Check with your brokerage's "International Operations" team. If they say "U.S. Notary required," go to the Consulate. If they say "Apostille is required," follow the MEA process.

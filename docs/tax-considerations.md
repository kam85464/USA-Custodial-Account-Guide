# Tax Considerations (U.S. → International)

## U.S. Side
- Dividends and capital gains may be taxable  
- **Kiddie Tax:** Unearned income above a certain threshold (e.g., $2,600) may be taxed at the parent's rate.
- Withdrawals themselves are not taxed  
- Keep Form 1099 for records  
- **Gift Tax:** Contributions are subject to annual exclusion limits ($18k/year per person).
- **W-8BEN:** After moving, file this form to reduce dividend withholding from 30% to 15% via the U.S.-India treaty.
- **Estate Tax:** U.S. assets over $60,000 held by non-residents may be subject to high U.S. estate taxes.

## Dual-Status Tax Years (The Year You Move)
If you relocate mid-year, you are considered a "Dual-Status" alien.
- **Kiddie Tax:** The $2,600 threshold (2024) applies to unearned income during the *resident* portion of the year.
- **Standard Deduction:** Dual-status taxpayers **cannot** claim the standard deduction. This often leads to a higher-than-expected tax bill for the custodial account.
- **Transition Point:** Income received after your departure date follows Non-Resident Alien (NRA) rules (30% flat or treaty rates).
- **Filing:** You must file a 1040 with "Dual-Status Statement" across the top, often accompanied by a 1040-NR.
- **Worksheet:** Use the [Dual-Status Tax Worksheet](./dual-status-worksheet.md) to track your income split.

## U.S. Exit Tax (Section 877A)
If you are a long-term resident (Green Card holder for at least 8 of the last 15 years), you may be subject to the Exit Tax upon surrendering your residency:
- **Covered Expatriate:** You trigger this if your net worth is >$2M, your average 5-year tax liability is above a certain threshold, or you cannot certify 5 years of tax compliance.
- **Mark-to-Market:** The IRS treats all your assets as if they were sold the day before you expatriated.
- **Exemption:** There is an exclusion amount for gains (e.g., $866,000 for 2024).
- **Planning:** Check your residency status and asset values before surrendering a Green Card to avoid being a "covered expatriate."

## Foreign / Resident Country Side
- Remittances may require local reporting  
- Capital gains may be taxable in your country of residence  
- **Foreign Asset Reporting:** Many countries (e.g., India's Schedule FA) require residents to report foreign assets.
- Keep documentation of:
  - Purchase price  
  - Sale price  
  - Transfer amounts  
- **Capital Controls:** Be aware of local rules (e.g., India's LRS/TCS) when funding the account from abroad.

## Double Taxation Avoidance (DTAA)
If the U.S. and your resident country have a tax treaty, you may be protected from paying full taxes in both countries:
- **Foreign Tax Credit (FTC):** You can often claim the taxes paid in the U.S. as a credit against your local tax liability for that same income.
- **Local Compliance:** Check if your country requires specific forms (e.g., India's Form 67) to claim this credit.
- **Reporting:** Ensure the gross income is reported locally, and the U.S. tax is deducted via the FTC section.

## Tax Treaty (W-8BEN) Benefits by Country
By filing Form W-8BEN, you notify the IRS that you are a tax resident of a treaty country. Typical rates for common countries:

| Country | Dividend Rate (with Treaty) | Common Local Reporting |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **United Kingdom** | 15% | Self-Assessment (Foreign Income Page) |
| **Canada** | 15% | T1 Income Tax Return (Foreign Property) |
| **Australia** | 15% | Individual Tax Return (Foreign Income) |
| **India** | 15% | ITR (Schedule FA and Form 67) |

## Tax-Efficient Patterns
- Long-term holding reduces taxable events  
- Use broad ETFs (tax-efficient)  
- Avoid frequent trading  
- Time withdrawals around tax years  

### Tax Loss Harvesting (Kiddie Tax Strategy)
If the custodial account's unearned income (dividends + realized gains) approaches the **$2,600 threshold**, use Tax Loss Harvesting to stay below the parent's tax rate:
- **Harvesting Losses:** Sell "loser" stocks/ETFs at a loss to offset realized capital gains.
- **The $2,600 Target:** Aim to keep net realized income below this limit to ensure the child pays the lower "child rate" rather than your higher marginal rate.
- **Wash Sale Rule:** Remember that you cannot buy the same or a "substantially identical" security within 30 days of the sale, or the tax loss will be disallowed by the IRS.

## Cost Basis and Residency Transition
When the beneficiary moves to a new country, the way their "Cost Basis" (purchase price) is calculated for local taxes may change:
- **U.S. Rule (Carryover Basis):** For UGMA/UTMA accounts, the child's cost basis is the same as the price at which the shares were originally purchased. There is **no** step-up in basis when the child takes ownership at age 18-25.
- **International "Deemed Acquisition":** Some countries (like Canada) treat you as having "bought" all your assets at the Fair Market Value (FMV) on the day you become a resident. This effectively provides a "step-up" for local tax purposes.
- **India/Other Countries:** Many countries do not provide a step-up and will use the original U.S. purchase price to calculate capital gains.
- **Strategy:** If your new country does not allow a step-up, consider if it is tax-advantageous to sell and immediately repurchase assets (realizing gains) while still in a lower U.S. tax bracket before becoming a resident of a high-tax country.

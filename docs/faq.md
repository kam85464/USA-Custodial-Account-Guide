# Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. What is the $60,000 U.S. Estate Tax risk?
For Non-Resident Aliens (NRAs), the U.S. imposes an estate tax on "U.S.-situated assets" (which includes stocks and ETFs of U.S. companies) if the total value exceeds **$60,000** at the time of the owner's death. 
*   **The Risk:** The tax rate starts high and can reach **40%** on the amount above $60k. This applies because the assets are physically located in the U.S.
*   **Mitigation:** Some parents purchase a small U.S. term life insurance policy to cover this potential tax hit, or they ensure the child understands the need to potentially liquidate or move assets before a status change, though this is complex for minors.
*   **Note:** Assets like U.S. bank deposits are generally exempt from this specific estate tax, but stocks/ETFs are not.

### 2. Can I use an Indian address for the account?
While some brokerages (like Schwab International) allow Indian addresses, standard domestic UGMA/UTMA accounts opened while a resident usually require a U.S. physical address to remain "active." Switching to an Indian address may result in:
*   The account being restricted to "liquidate only" (no new buying).
*   The account being closed if the broker does not support residents of India.

### 3. Does the "Kiddie Tax" apply if we live in India?
Yes, if the child is still considered a U.S. person for tax purposes (e.g., a U.S. Citizen) or has U.S. source income. Even as an NRA, U.S. source unearned income is subject to U.S. taxation, though the W-8BEN form helps manage the rates via the treaty.

### 4. What happens to the U.S. bank account?
Most U.S. banks require a domestic address to keep the account open. If you close your U.S. bank account, funding or withdrawing from the custodial account becomes significantly harder and more expensive (wire transfers).

### 5. What happens if the Custodian becomes incapacitated or passes away?
This is a major risk when living abroad. If no **Successor Custodian** is named, a U.S. court may need to appoint one, which is nearly impossible to manage from India.
*   **Successor Custodian:** Most brokerages (Fidelity/Schwab) allow you to designate a successor custodian. This person would automatically take over management of the account for the minor without needing a court order.
*   **If the Successor lives abroad:** If the successor does not have a U.S. residential address, the brokerage may restrict the account to "liquidate-only" mode or require the assets to be moved to an International-specific account (like Schwab International) upon the successor taking control. 
*   **Identity Verification:** A successor living abroad will likely need to provide documents notarized at a U.S. Consulate to prove their identity before the brokerage grants access.
*   **Action:** Always name a spouse or trusted U.S.-based relative as the successor.

### 6. Is an Indian "Power of Attorney" valid for my U.S. account?
Generally, **no**. U.S. financial institutions are extremely hesitant to accept foreign legal documents.
*   **Validation:** An Indian PoA would likely require an **Apostille** (an international certification) to be even considered.
*   **The Better Way:** Use the brokerage's own specific "Durable Power of Attorney" or "Successor Custodian" forms. If you are already in India, you may need to visit a U.S. Consulate to get these forms notarized for them to be accepted.

### 7. Security & 2FA: Google Voice vs. Physical SIM?
Maintaining access to U.S. SMS codes from India is critical.
*   **Google Voice (VoIP):** 
    *   *Pros:* Free, works over internet. 
    *   *Cons:* Many U.S. banks (and some brokerages) block "VoIP" numbers for 2FA for security reasons. It is risky to rely on this as your sole 2FA method.
*   **Physical SIM (e.g., Tello, Ultra Mobile):** 
    *   *Pros:* These are "real" mobile numbers (non-VoIP) and are 100% compatible with bank 2FA. 
    *   *Cons:* Requires a monthly fee (~$5-10) and a phone that supports Wi-Fi calling to receive SMS in India without massive roaming charges.

### 8. What is the "Sailing Permit" (Form 1040-C)?
Technically called the "Departing Alien Income Tax Return," it is a certificate of compliance from the IRS.
*   **Purpose:** To prove to the U.S. government that you have paid all taxes due before leaving.
*   **Timing:** You should file this no earlier than 30 days before you leave, and at least 14 days before departure.
*   **Reality:** While Customs and Border Protection (CBP) rarely asks for it at the airport, having it is legally required and prevents issues if you ever apply for a U.S. visa or Green Card in the future.

### 9. How does "Dual-Status" affect the account?
In the year you move, your tax residency changes.
*   **Residency Split:** You are taxed as a resident until the day you leave and a non-resident after.
*   **Wash Sale Rule:** If you sell a stock for a loss to stay under the Kiddie Tax threshold, you cannot buy it back within 30 days. This applies even if you move countries during that 30-day window.

### 10. Can my child manage the account from abroad?
Yes. Once the child reaches the age of majority (18-25 depending on the state), they can complete the "Transfer of Assets" process online or via phone from their new country. They will likely need to submit a W-8BEN in their own name at that time.
